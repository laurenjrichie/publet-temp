Introduction and Objectives

Thomas Edison. Henry Ford. Harlan Sanders. Walt Disney. Steve Jobs.

Sam Walton.

For us...especially Sam Walton.

What all these names have in common is their status as icons of business – innovators, achievers and real-life American dream success stories. Perhaps more than any of them, Sam Walton represents the best in all of us, the potential that lies within each and every person who strives to do their best...no matter how humble their beginnings...regardless of where they’re from.
I’ve often heard that your brand is what someone says about you when you’re not in the room. Despite his being gone from us over 20 years, the strength of Sam Walton’s brand is what people say not just of him but also what he taught us. What people say when you’re not in the room is one thing. What people say when you’re no longer on this earth is an even stronger indication of one’s personal brand.

Sam Walton meant a great deal to millions of people. The stories I’ve been fortunate to hear from those who knew Sam personally paint a picture of a man who, despite his enormous success, never lost sight of what really mattered – people. Whether associates, customers, suppliers, even competitors, Sam Walton was able to bring out the best in them because he made the people around him feel like he was indeed one them, nothing more and nothing less. Just how true that was, I’ll leave up to you, the student of Sam Walton’s life, his legacy and his brand.

And so, with this guide, our purpose is not to stifle creativity or to be rigid and controlling. Instead, it is to be protective of the personal brand that Sam built and to preserve how Sam would have wanted to be remembered.

I would like to be remembered as a good friend. That’s important. I have such strong feelings for the folks in our company. They have meant so much to me.

- Sam Walton

Let these guidelines and these words from Sam help you navigate the creation of communications worthy of his legacy. Ready to get started? Give me a “W”!

Alan Dranow
Senior Director: Heritage and Associate Marketing

When to Use this Guide

The brand guidelines included here address references, quotes, written works, photography, video, audio – everything we could think of that associates like you might want to access and use for internal purposes.

Meetings. Inspiration. Posters. Videos. Presentations. If we missed something, just submit a request and let us know. We’ll be happy to take a closer look at granting special permission for use as appropriate.

Around here, he continues to be especially popular. Processing requests to use Sam Walton-related images and resources is a lofty task, time consuming and, at peak times, overwhelming. Referring to Sam Walton

What’s in a name? Some would say everything. And Sam Walton’s name falls in to this all-encompassing category. Serving as a strong reminder of his legacy, it reflects the integrity, leadership, vision and desire for excellence in serving others that he was so committed to. By acknowledging and recognizing the importance of his name, we are able to show continued respect when we use it appropriately as outlined here.

Preferred Use of His Name

Sam. It’s straightforward, uncomplicated, likeable and personal. Simply put, Sam just felt being on a first-name basis with everyone was the best way to show respect for associates at all levels within the company – hourly, management, stores, distribution centers, warehouse clubs. For him, there wasn’t room for a Mr., Mrs. or Miss on a name badge, even on his own. Why? Sam felt it stood in the way of communicating effectively, building partnerships and practicing servant leadership.

In case you didn’t the memo in 1985, here it is...with all of the important details highlighted.

Acceptable Uses of His Name

Of course, there are additional ways to reference Sam’s name. Over the years, Sam Walton and Sam M. Walton have been used frequently, especially when crediting him with a quote or written work. Many in the region closest to the company’s roots affectionately refer to him as Mr. Sam, a reference that respectfully blends both formality and familiarity. And the traditional use of Mr. Walton offered those who preferred a bit more common formality another way of addressing him and showing their respect.

Make note that Samuel M. Walton and Samuel Moore Walton are also acceptable ways used to refer to Sam as well. However, they are only used externally, never internally, unless special permission is granted by the Walmart Heritage Group (????@wal-mart.com).

Unacceptable Uses of His Name

There are several preferred and acceptable ways to use Sam’s name. Likewise, there are many that aren’t acceptable to use since they don’t appropriately reflect Sam’s personal brand. These are some examples of those that don’t that should be avoided – Sammy, Samuel, Mr. Walmart, Sam Walmart, Sammy Walmart, Sammy Boy. Inquiries about using any names not specifically called out in this guide should be directed to the Walmart Heritage Group (????@wal-mart.com) for approval.

Sam Walton

(1918-1992)

Sam Walton was born in 1918 in Kingfisher, Oklahoma. In 1942, he joined the military and married Helen Robson on Valentine’s Day of the following year. When his military service ended in 1945, Sam and Helen moved to Iowa and then to Newport, Arkansas. During this time, Sam gained his early retail experience, eventually operating his own variety store along the way before Sam and his family would leave Newport for Bentonville, where he and Helen would raise their four children – Rob, John, Jim and Alice – and he would open Walton’s 5&10 on the downtown square.

Inspired by the early success of his dime store, and driven to bring even greater opportunity and value to his customers, Sam opened the first Walmart in 1962 in Rogers, Arkansas, at the age of 44. While competitors thought his idea that a successful business could be built around offering lower prices and great service would never work, it turned out the company’s success exceeded even Sam’s expectations. Walmart went public in 1970, and the proceeds financed a steady expansion of the business.

Sam credited the rapid growth of Walmart not just to the low costs that attracted his customers, but also to his associates. He relied on them to give customers the great shopping experience that would keep them coming back. Sam shared his vision for the company with associates in a way that was nearly unheard of in the industry. He made them partners in the success of the company, and he firmly believed that this partnership was what made Walmart great.

As the stores grew, so did Sam’s aspirations. In addition to bringing new approaches and technologies to retail, he also experimented with new store formats and even made the decision to take Walmart into Mexico. Sam’s fearlessness in offering lower prices and bringing Walmart’s value to customers in the U.S. and beyond set a standard for the company that lives on to this day. His strong commitment to service and to the values that help individuals, businesses and the country succeed earned him the Presidential Medal of Freedom, awarded by President George H. W. Bush in 1992.

It was during Sam’s acceptance remarks that he articulated what would come to be Walmart’s official company purpose.

If we work together, we’ll lower the cost of living for everyone...we’ll give the world an opportunity to see what it’s like to save and have a better life.

Today, “saving people money so they can live better” is the driving force behind everything the company does.

Sam Walton died in 1992, shortly after receiving the Medal of Freedom, but his legacy lives on. To this day, Walmart remains a leader in the retail industry. It’s committed not just to expanding the business to better serve its customers but also to improving the communities it serves through their efforts to constantly improve what they do and how they do it.


Using Sam's Quotes and Written Works

Sam was very passionate about what he did, and it showed...in all of his actions and words. Committed to the company and, more importantly, its associates, he embraced both the successes and failures encountered.

They influenced his vision. They impacted his business decisions. And, whether his resulting words were delivered at a Saturday Morning Meeting, an impromptu store visit or on a weekend hunting trip, they were often at the heart of the wisdom he strived to pass on to others from the moment he opened the store’s doors.

Best Practices for Usage

Want to use Sam’s words of business wisdom to build a case for new ideas? To support and reinforce existing ideas? To motivate fellow associates? The right quote can add or subtract value in the areas of impact, interest and effectiveness to any idea(s) being presented and shared.

Quoting Sam effectively, appropriately and respectively is a matter of keeping these things in mind:

Sam’s words should...

1. Be used only in the context in which they were intended. This helps ensure they convey his thoughts in a manner reflective of what he truly intended.

2. Be used for internal, business-related situations only and chosen carefully for relevance and relation to the subject being addressed.

3. Be used in a positive manner – to emphasize, reinforce, support and inspire. They should never be misconstrued or used in ways that are perceived as negative, chastising or demeaning.

4. Be credited to him...always. When quoting him, be sure to use quotation marks and acknowledge he is the source for the words of wisdom you are sharing with others.

Sam’s 10 Rules for Building a Business

Source: Walton, Sam and John Huey. “Sam Walton: Made in America.” New York: Doubleday, 1992. Pgs. 246–249.

Sam’s Most Frequently Quoted Topics

In our stores, clubs, distribution centers and offices, Sam’s quotes appear all around us – on the walls and on the WIRE, at the meetings and at the heart of most things we do. Some of the most popular quotes we see are taken from Sam’s 10 Rules for Building a Business. We have included those here.

 Rule 1. COMMIT to your business.

Believe in it more than anybody else. I think I overcame every single one of my personal shortcomings by the sheer passion I brought to my work. I don’t know if you’re born with this kind of passion, or if you can learn it. But I do know you need it. If you love your work, you’ll be out there every day trying to do it the best you possibly can, and pretty soon everybody around will catch the passion from you – like a fever.

 Rule 2. SHARE your profits with your associates, and treat them as partners.

In turn, they will treat you as a partner, and together you will all perform beyond your wildest expectations. Remain a corporation and retain control if you like, but behave as a servant leader in a partnership. Encourage your associates to hold a stake in the company. Offer discounted stock, and grant them stock for their retirement. It’s the single best thing we ever did.

 Rule 3. MOTIVATE your partners.

Money and ownership alone aren’t enough. Constantly, day by day, think of new and more interesting ways to motivate and challenge your partners. Set high goals, encourage competition, and then keep score. Make bets with outrageous payoffs. If things get stale, cross-pollinate; have managers switch jobs with one another to stay challenged. Keep everybody guessing as to what your next trick is going to be. Don’t become too predictable.

 Rule 4. COMMUNICATE everything you possibly can to your partners.

The more they know, the more they’ll understand. The more they understand, the more they’ll care. Once they care, there’s no stopping them. If you don’t trust your associates to know what’s going on, they’ll know you don’t really consider them partners. Information is power, and the gain you get from empowering your associates more than offsets the risk of informing your competitors.

 Rule 5. APPRECIATE everything your associates do for the business.

A paycheck and a stock option will buy one kind of loyalty. But all of us like to be told how much somebody appreciates what we do for them. We like to hear it often, and especially when we have done something we’re really proud of. Nothing else can quite substitute for a few well-chosen, well-timed, sincere words of praise. They’re absolutely free – and worth a fortune.

 Rule 6. CELEBRATE your success.

Find some humor in your failures. Don’t take yourself so seriously. Loosen up, and everybody around you will loosen up. Have fun. Show enthusiasm – always. When all else fails, put on a costume and sing a silly song. Then make everybody else sing with you. Don’t do a hula on Wall Street. It’s been done. All of this is important, and more fun than you think, and it really fools competition. “Why should we take those cornballs at Wal-Mart seriously?”

 Rule 7. LISTEN to everyone in your company.

And figure out ways to get them talking. The folks on the front lines – the ones who actually talk to the customer – are the only ones who really know what’s going on out there. You’d better find out what they know. This really is what total quality is all about. To push responsibility down in your organization, and to force good ideas to bubble up within it, you must listen to what your associates are trying to tell you.

 Rule 8. EXCEED your customers' expectations.

If you do, they’ll come back over and over. Give them what they want – and a little more. Let them know you appreciate them. Make good on all your mistakes, and don’t make excuses – apologize. Stand behind everything you do. The two most important words I ever wrote were on that first Wal-Mart sign: “Satisfaction Guaranteed.” They’re still up there, and they have made all the difference.

 Rule 9. CONTROL your expenses better than your competition.

This is where you can always find the competitive advantage. For twenty-five years running – long before Wal-Mart was known as the nation’s largest retailer – we ranked number one in our industry for the lowest ratio of expenses to sales. You can make a lot of different mistakes and still recover if you run an efficient operation. Or you can be brilliant and still go out of business if you’re too inefficient.

 Rule 10. SWIM upstream.

Go the other way. Ignore conventional wisdom. If everybody else is doing it one way, there’s a good chance you can find your niche by going in exactly the opposite direction. But be prepared for a lot of folks to wave you down and tell you you’re headed the wrong way. I guess in all my years, what I heard more often than anything was: a town of less than 50,000 population cannot support a discount store for very long.

Using Photographic Images of Sam

Just look around you. Chances are you’ll see a photo of Sam somewhere close by. On bended knee with yellow pad in hand. Hula dancing on Wall Street. Donning his trademark trucker-style cap. Accepting the nation’s highest civilian honor with pride. These pictures tell a story about the life of a man always mindful of making every day a milestone for the people around him. That was Sam’s legacy.

We are fortunate to have countless pictures of Sam to serve as reminders of his life, his hard work, his amazing accomplishments. And it’s important that we continue honoring him by only using images that show him doing what he did best. In our book, it’s the only way to do it simply because that’s the way Sam would have wanted it.

Helen Robson Walton (1919-2007)

Helen Walton was born in 1919 in Claremore, Oklahoma. In April of 1942, after graduating from the University of Oklahoma with a degree in finance, she met Sam Walton and married him shortly thereafter on Valentine’s Day 1943. Together, they would raise their four children ­– Rob, John, Jim and Alice – in Bentonville, Arkansas, where she and Sam would open Walton’s 5&10 on the downtown square.

In addition to being Sam’s partner in life, Helen was also considered by Sam to be one of his best business advisors. Sam credited her for the original idea for the Walmart profit-sharing plan, which gives all associates an opportunity to share in the profits of the company. 

But Helen was also her own force to be reckoned with as a leader on the philanthropic front. Driven by a passion to improve the lives of those less fortunate than she, she often prefaced her comments about an important cause by saying, “We knew we had to do something.” She focused her charitable giving on a variety of causes, including education, the arts, families and children and she was very active with the Presbyterian Church (USA) at local and national levels. 

While serving as president of the Walton Family Foundation, the largest gift ever made to a public university in the US – $300 million – was provided to the University of Arkansas. She was a supporter of the Walton Arts Center. She served on the Governor’s Task Force on Child Care and the Governor’s Commission on Adult Literacy. And she was elected the first woman President of the Presbyterian Church (USA) Foundation in its almost 200-year history. These examples are only a few of the many ways Helen gave back to others as a volunteer to her community, state and nation.

On behalf of the Walton family, she accepted the national Patriot Award in 1997, presented by the Congressional Medal of Honor Society – the first time in the Society’s history the award has been presented to a family. She was recognized for her philanthropy and service to education in the state and named one of the top 100 Women in Arkansas five years in a row. And, she and Sam received the Order of the Quetzal (highest civilian award) in Guatemala for service in education to the young people of the country and for mission help among Indian tribes. 

After a lifetime devoted to helping others and to improving the quality of life in Northwest Arkansas, Helen passed away in 2007 at the age of 87.

S. Robson “Rob” Walton (1944– )

Rob, the oldest son of Sam Walton, joined the company in 1969. Prior to becoming chairman, he held a variety of positions with Walmart, including senior vice president, corporate secretary, general counsel and vice chairman. He joined the company’s board of directors in 1978. He has held his current position as Walmart’s Chairman of the Board of Directors since 1992. And he currently serves on both the Executive and Global Compensation committees.

Before joining Walmart, Rob was a partner with the law firm of Conner & Winters in Tulsa, Oklahoma. He is currently involved with a number of nonprofit and educational organizations, including Conservation International, where he serves as chairman of the executive committee, and the College of Wooster, where he is an Emeritus Life Trustee.

John Thomas Walton (1946–2005)

John, the second son of Sam Walton, was a Green Beret United States war veteran. Upon his return from Vietnam, he learned to fly and worked as a pilot for Walmart before leaving the company to co-found Satloc, an aerial application company that pioneered the use of GPS technology in agricultural crop dusting. From there he went on to found Corsair Marine and serve as the chairman of venture capital firm, True North Partners.

Along with the help of friend Ted Forstmann, John established the Children’s Scholarship Fund to provide tuition assistance for low-income families so they could send their children to private schools. He was awarded the William E. Simmons Prize for Philanthropic Leadership for his achievements.

Unfortunately, John’s life was lost in 2005 when the homebuilt CGS Hawk Arrow aircraft he was piloting (registered as an “experimental aircraft” per FAA regulations) crashed shortly after takeoff.

Alice Louise Walton (1949– )

Alice Walton is the only daughter and the youngest of Sam’s four children. A graduate of  Trinity College in San Antonio, Texas, she secured a B.A. in Economics and Finance and  started her career in finance as an equity analyst and money manager for First Commerce Corporation. Alice later went on to serve as Vice Chairman and head of all investment-related activities at the Arvest Bank Group. And, in 1988, she founded Llama Company, an investment bank engaged in corporate finance, public and structured finance, real estate finance and sales and trading, serving as President, Chairman and CEO. Currently, she lives in central Texas, where she raises cutting horses and operates the Rocking W Ranch.

In addition to her philanthropic activities as a board member of the Walton Family Foundation, Ms. Walton has been active on the Board of the University of Arkansas for Medical Sciences at Little Rock and the Board of Advisors for the University of Arkansas Graduate Business School at Fayetteville where the Alice L. Walton Chair in Finance was established in 1996, allowing the University, through its College of Business Administration, to pursue educational excellence on a national and international level.

Outside of the classroom, Alice’s vision led to the creation of Camp War Eagle, a unique summer camp in Northwest Arkansas that brings together children of differing socio-economic backgrounds and provides a remarkable experience for children who might not otherwise have the opportunity to attend a camp of this stature.

An avid supporter of the arts, Alice serves on the board of the Amon Carter Museum in Fort Worth, Texas, and is a member of the Trustees’ Council of the National Gallery of Art in Washington, DC. In addition, she was the driving force leading the Walton Family Foundation to develop Crystal Bridges – Museum of American Art, a premier national art institution venue dedicated to American art and artists and a place of learning and community. She is the museum’s Chairman of the Board.

James Carr “Jim” Walton (1948– )

Jim, Sam Walton’s youngest son, joined Walmart’s Board of Directors in 2005 and serves on the Executive committee. In addition to banking and investment expertise, his leadership positions at the companies he has led have given him extensive knowledge in the areas of executive leadership, strategic planning and management.
Jim is chairman and CEO of Arvest Bank Group, Inc., a group of banks operating in Arkansas, Kansas, Missouri and Oklahoma. And he is also chairman of Community Publishers, Inc., which operates newspapers in Arkansas, Missouri and Oklahoma.

James Lawrence “Bud” Walton (1921–1995)

The entry of Sam Walton’s younger brother, Bud, into the retail industry was largely because of Sam, who convinced him to become a J.C. Penney trainee after Sam had started up with the company himself. Eventually, Bud would help Sam to open the first Walmart Discount City in Rogers, Arkansas, in 1962. And he would also continue to work with his brother and serve the company as a senior vice president and director, specializing in obtaining real estate and constructing store buildings.

On the philanthropic front, Bud donated $15 million to the University of Arkansas for the construction of a basketball arena, which is named in his honor. And the College of the Ozarks in Point Lookout, Missouri, is home to the James L.“Bud”Walton Chair of Retailing, named in honor of Bud’s support for the school.
